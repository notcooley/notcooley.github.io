## Γειά σου Aidan,

That’s Greek for “hello,” (pronounced ya-soo) at least according to Google Translate. My coworker Doug will probably flog me over the head for using such a service.

As you well know, I graduated college two Fridays ago (at 8 AM, mind you, which made me a very bleary Levi indeed). I might talk more about that on a later date, but for now, my thoughts on it are much too abstract and knotted to mold into coherent sentences. To celebrate, my parents and I decided to go to Capitol Reef National Park, a park none of us have been to despite spending significant chunks of time in Utah. After the convocation, and a shotgun suit fitting, which felt very nearly like what I imagine being on an operating table feels like, we set the Steed’s front bumper due south and made our way. 

And I slept. For like 2 hours. I’m not kidding about the 8 AM convocation, a time that’s no problem for you, but as you know, quite a monumental task for me. I woke up for 10 minutes to have a sleepy conversation with my sister, and then fell asleep again before opening my eyes just in time to weigh in on whether we should stop and see Pando or not. It was on the way, and we hadn’t been in a long time, so we decided to make the stop.

I’ve been to Pando as a wee lad, but I don’t remember it at all. I thought it would be in a single, demarcated area, but boy was I wrong. It expands and expands and expands. I honestly couldn’t tell if the forest was entirely Pando or if Pando merges with a forest of another kind at some point. It was a behemoth. I found out later, thanks to a nifty little pamphlet that I’ll have to fish from the trash, that it spreads over 106 acres and weighs 13.2 million pounds. Million. That’s just insane. To think one organism can be so large honestly makes my brain hurt a little. And you can feel it. Standing in Pando, staring at the eyes in its trunks, feels different. Maybe it was just placebo, but I felt connected to Pando in a way I haven’t felt with other forests (it’s okay to cringe at that statement, I did too writing it). I took a whizz on a particularly appealing trunk. Now the minerals of my piss are forever entwined with Pando’s life story.

If you haven’t been to Pando, you should go. It was beautiful, even with the leaves dressing the forest floor rather than the trees themselves. I’m sure it’s stunning in the fall.

After the diversion, we continued on our way, made it to our cute little motel, and went to sleep. (I promise I didn’t sleep the whole time, it just so happens that I’m mentioning it a lot. I just woke up, so sleep is on the mind).

We slept in a bit on Saturday, leaving for Capitol Reef around 10 AM. And boy, what a beautiful drive! What was obscured by the night was made beautifully apparent by the clarity of morning. Green-gray shrubs made dappled constellations on the painterly red cliffs that loomed on either side of the road. Wispy clouds ambled across the sky, doubtless pointing out shapes they saw in the humans passing underneath. It had been quite some time since I had seen red rock of this kind. It’s different from that of Vegas, I think. More unassuming, maybe.

We spent the day hiking. Our first stop was to a formation called the Tanks, three tiered pools of water located off to the left of what I think is considered a slot canyon. Next was Cassidy Arch, named after the infamous Butch Cassidy (who only supposedly passed through the area). The hike was a decently tough one, but my parents pushed through. My mom has become a much better hiker. The arch itself was unlike any I have seen. That’s not to say it’s distinctly beautiful, but it’s also not to say that it’s not stunning, because it is; it’s just a unique formation. The top of this bluff you get onto slopes into a deep pit, and the arch is essentially a hole in the side of this pit, revealing the open air behind. It was rad. 

Walking onto the arch revealed a set of chains and six bolts right next to each other. I assume they’re for repelling in case someone falls in, but all I could think about was what the climbing in the pit was like. In fact, much of what I was thinking about while in Capitol Reef was what the climbing was like. There were tons of boulder fields that I’m sure are mostly choss but might be hiding a gem or two.

Our last hike was to an arch called Hickman Bridge. This one was a stunner. You should look up a picture of it. The hike was a lot easier, but you get to hike under the bridge, which is cool. Along the way, there was a craggly rock face with far-from-ancient hand prints absolutely covering it (surely some slightly self-oblivious youth group). I don’t know if you’ve seen Brother Bear, but it reminded me of that. And there were so many lizards. Lizards were common throughout Capitol Reef, but on this hike especially.

The interesting thing about Capitol Reef is that there’s a little town in it. It’s not inhabited by permanent residents anymore, of course, it’s just a historic site, but back in the late 1800s and early 1900s, it was a quaint Mormon settlement. Sulphur Creek and Fremont River, which contributed to the formation of Capitol Reef, irrigated their crops and orchards. The oldest remaining building is now a bakery where we got some freshly made pies. I got some homemade barbecue sauce and habañero peach jam, the latter of which I’ve been gorging myself on for the past three days (but both are delectable).

I’ve moved on to the next day without realizing it. One last thing about the day before, when we did all the hikes: we got some great burgers from the food truck outside our motel (I relaxed a bit on the whole not eating red meat thing that day) and then watched The Dark Wizard, which my parents subtly lost their minds over. And one more last thing: we kept seeing these perfectly round holes in the sandstone in Capitol Reef, usually in groupings of six and about two inches deep. This leads me to believe there is a six-toed beast that roams the park at night, shifting the sandstone as it walks and feasting on unassuming desert lizards. Dessert lizards, if you will. But I have no proof of this.

Now that Saturday is sufficiently recapped, I can truly do the next day justice. The only thing I want to mention is this beautiful view we saw of the Goosenecks, past Panoramic Point. The canyon walls had three layers of rock, each from different geological epochs, the bottom of which is the same age and rock as the top layer of the Grand Canyon. Isn’t that crazy? We stayed there for a solid twenty minutes and watched the swallows chase each other around, catching bugs mid-flight. They’re quite fast.

If you ever get the chance to go to Capitol Reef, whether it’s with Emily or your family or by yourself or whatever, go. It’s peaceful there (or about as peaceful as it can be in a National Park these days). You’ll love it. I hope Greece is just as beautiful and pleasant as your pictures make it seem.
