## Hello Levi, 

We are concluding our second full day in Athens, it has been a super exciting experience. While I likely cannot do it all justice, I’ve chosen to step through the events chronologically in hopes of capturing all there is. At a high level the biggest takeaways are that A) I was very mislead in my knowledge about Ancient Greece and B) My biggest fear might very well be being a filthy American in another country. 

What might make this piece more exciting is that we didn’t actually go to the Acropolis Museum, so I get to fill in the gaps with speculation and the occasional quick Google search. This is not to say that I’m totally in the dark. I am my grandparents grandson, so I read every single plaque in full. This might have bothered Emily but luckily she was equally distracted by the stray cats that sat atop most marble columns and limestone blocks throughout Athens. 

![Emily heads through the gates](images/emily_sun.JPG)

After a good night's rest in our hostel, we got a late start in the busy streets of downtown Psiri. Our first stop was right across the street to the nearest cafe. We were immediately clocked as Americans and were told we needed to try their cappuccino – it wasn’t good. Well it was an ok drink but it definitely wasn’t a good cappuccino. I also ordered two and they gave us one, and I was too awkward to go back in and make the correction. 

Coffee in hand we moved along into the square beneath the Acropolis. Alleys shot East and Westward into the Athens Flea Market, while the South climbed narrow streets towards Ancient Greece. In the morning we quickly noticed the gridlocked lines of sunbaked tourists slugging up towards the Parthenon, and through the freedom of minimal planning chose to tour the surrounding grounds instead. Our first stop was the Prison of Socrates, a cavern that to our surprise didn’t house the Philosopher, nor was it a prison at all. The cave was actually used to hide artifacts from the Acropolis Museum during WWII. 

We made our way down to the Pnyx (I don’t know how to pronounce it either). This outcropping lies beneath the Hill of the Muses and is the birthplace of Democracy. It is also the place where my perception of Ancient Greece began to change. See, I thought Ancient Greece was a country, but it was actually a collection of city states that dotted the coasts of the Aegean Sea, Athens and Sparta the primary two. While Sparta maintained a tight military rule and the Persians to the East had enough resources to hold an absolute monarchy, the Athenians with fewer resources and a large peasant population couldn’t make it either. The rich needed the poor to function, and had to make concessions to do so. In consequence, on the Pnyx, the people began to gather, argue, and vote on the future of the city. While anyone with half a brain might look at the state of our democracy with distaste, the Athenians would see even the most well oiled parliament as an oligarchy. This is because in Ancient Greece, every citizen had an equal voice and vote. Of course that didn’t count women, slaves or migrant workers, but I digress. 

![Sitting below the Parthenon](images/parthanon_corner.JPG)

We then made the climb up the Hill of The Muses where another monument to Philopappus sits. Philopappus was actually a Roman Prince who died in 116 AD meaning that this monument came at least 600 odd years after Pnyx began seeing traffic. The timeline here in Athens is what fascinates me the most. While Athens came under the reigns of several different rulers, the Athenian culture and these monolithic monuments persist through them all.

After lunch we went to the Agora. Sleeping under the poppy fields, olive trees, and friendly cats lays the remains of a booming city center. The Temple of Hephaestus is the most prominent building here. Construction began in the 400 BCs and was originally in honor of the Olympian for which it’s named. Interestingly as Athens saw rulers come and go, they basically just changed what the temple was dedicated to. This to me is a funny theme you’ll see throughout Athens. Yes the Orthodoxy built churches as they took up occupation, but are you going to destroy the pretty marble building across the field? No, just say that it’s for Jesus and move on. In fact, under the Ottomans who created the first modern Greek state, the temple became a mosque. If anything, grounds this diverse should harbor the Unitarians. 

On another interesting note, the temple of Apollo was much larger than the one to Zeus and Athena. This seems like a sentiment shared in much of the classical Greek literature I’ve read. These guys loved Apollo and sort of just tolerated the king and queen of the universe. 

By far the funniest fact we learned was on how this fledgling democracy chose jurors. By using a large stone tablet with a matrix of protruding sticks, and dropping a ball down from the top, they could randomly choose the jurors for a given case. That’s right, to bring stability to the justice system the Greeks created plinko! While jury duty is a mundane task in modern America kicked off by a dreaded letter in the mail, if I got to play plinko, I’d be psyched to sit on that bench. 

![The Temple of Nike](images/nike.JPG) 

Another peculiarity we learned here was in the process of making sculptures. The Agora Museum housed a couple of partially done pieces. At first I thought they were just ordinary busts as a complete head sat atop an otherwise blank stone pillar. Then Emily observed that along with a completed head they had a completed manhood — I guess we know where the artist's priorities were.

The second day started on a miserable note. I thought we’d dodged jet lag since the first day went so swimmingly. But lying open eyed on the bed from 2 am on told a different story. By 5 am I was ready to fight fitful sleep and be cranky all day. Luckily my girlfriend is stellar, and suggested we bite the bullet, get up early, and hit the Acropolis first thing in the morning. We were at the gate, and before they opened the line of people stretched out of sight. Luckily for us, our youthful knees made for a quick flight ahead of the gaggle of retirees forming on our tail. This partnered with simply going a slightly different direction that the pack made it almost feel like we had the site to ourselves! 

The Acropolis is insane. What is left of it is magical, and knowing what once was is a thing of grandeur. Supposedly there sat a bronze statue of Athena so large you could see it from the sea. At its peak, the Parthenon and the Temple of Nike must have been incredible. We once again see empire after empire reusing sacred monuments. Alexander the Great, the first person to unite Greece under the Macedonian Empire, displayed his shields on the Parthenon. The Romans, when it was their turn to rule over Athens, constructed more statues of their ruling class and Nero added another stage to the Theatre of Dionysos.

The most impressive part of the Acropolis is just how long it has stood. I thought the Parthenon would have begun falling apart in the early ADs, but to my surprise it wasn’t until 1687 during the Morean War when it sustained a bulk of the damage. Renovation effort began in the early 20th century, but people naively thought that their modern construction standards would be enough, resulting in more damage done to the monument. However, in the 80’s with a better knowledge of the limestone, another round of renovations proved more successful. The coolest part of these renovations that I’ve never noticed from pictures is that they replaced every missing piece of marble with a new one, resulting in a bit of a jigsaw between the old rock and the new.

Anyways, Emily and I are awaiting our bus to head to Delphi after a less than smooth day of traveling. I’ll update you soon.
