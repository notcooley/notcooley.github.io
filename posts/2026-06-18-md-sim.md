# What is an Molecular Dynamic Simulation:

To my limited knowledge an MD-sim is just a way to represent some number of particles, in some number of dimensions, in a space of some size, bouncing off one another. There are all sorts of hyperparameters you can tweak to make it do what you want, but in essence, that’s it. A particle’s attributes (position, velocity, acceleration) are dependent upon the force being exerted on it. The force is dependent upon the proximity of the other particles in relation to a given particle. 

That force can be summed up by this graph. Close to 0 on the left, the particles are so close that they actually push each other away. In the well that dips below the x-axis they are in equilibrium, after that they are attracted to one another, and as you continue rightward they don’t really interact.

# Hurdles in an MD-sim:

As you can imagine there are a lot of things that can go wrong. Let’s first consider the time step we spoke of earlier, mainly what it should be. Particles start out far from each other (ideally), thus their potentials are negligible (right side of the graph). Pick a time step too small, and you’ll basically be watching paint dry. No particles will move, nothing fun will happen. Pick too large a time step and we end up on the left side of the graph. The particles have collided (if not moved through each other), and jettisoned away from one another, your sim will likely break. 

The next hurdle is one of runtime complexity. The naive would say, “If my force is dependent upon the force that all other particles exert upon me, I simply need to sum up all the forces acting on each particle for all particles.” The issue, that it runs in O(N^2) or Slow(N^2) as I’ve begun to call it. Your saving grace is that most particles aren’t that close to every other particle, and the force they exert on a distant particle is basically 0. So, our solution will be to decide which neighboring particles are close enough for us to care.

The last hurdle we look to solve is all the compute power involved. We want to look at a ton of particles over a ton of timesteps and we don’t want to wait for hours, if not days, to see the results. All of this will be addressed in due course.

# The Data Structure:

For me, making the data structure might have been the most fun part. A smart data structure helps to address the 2nd and 3rd issues above. With direction from staff we learned that if you split up the space into a grid, and only take into account the particles in your 3x3 space, the runtime drops to ~O(NlogN). 

To make this happen, you basically spit up your space of width *w* and height *h* into even box sizes. For example if you had *w*=*h*=10 and a box size of 2, you’d end up with a 5x5 grid. Then for a given particle you can figure out what box it should go into with:

	int x_indx = (int)(x_position / BOX_SIZE);
	
    int y_indx = (int)(y_position/ BOX_SIZE);


Now you may then ask, what if multiple particles land in the same box? That is where our 3rd dimension comes in (or 4th if your space is 3D). We make a doubly-linked-list that stores each particle in a box. The box actually stores a node that is the head of the list, and that node points to the next and so on. Think of each node as a container. It holds three things: the particle, the node after it, and the node before it. 

Summary: to find a particle

Take its position

Calculate the grid space it would be in 

Traverse down the linked list of nodes until you find it 

# Data Storage:

Now I haven’t been entirely truthful with you. The node doesn’t store the particle, it actually stores a reference to the particle. All the particles are then stored in a separate array elsewhere. At first that might sound more difficult because on paper it looks like you have to wander your CPU to find a particle; box to node and to particle seems inefficient. To fully understand the beauty of it we must first understand how your computer grabs memory.

# The Cache:

When a process asks your computer for data, the CPU grabs a chunk of memory and brings it into the cache. The cache is a small piece of SRAM memory that your CPU keeps near and dear to its heart because it is super fast to access. There are usually three layers, the L1, L2 and L3 cache. Each one is bigger and thus slower to read than the last. 

So back to the data your process needed. The CPU grabbed it from memory, which took a long time. But now it sits in the cache so if you need it again, it is right there. But as I alluded to earlier, your CPU grabbed a *chunk* of memory, not just the thing you wanted. So you can imagine if you have two pieces of data you’d like to access near each other in *time*, it pays to store them near each other in *memory*. 

If I want an apple today and an orange tomorrow, I don’t need to go to the store twice. I grab them both, eat an apple now, and I have the orange near by for later.

# Replace Fruit With Particles:

Back to the issue at hand; when I want one particle, I will always want others. But which others? Given a particle p, I only care to look at all the particles in p’s direct vicinity. Now let’s say that *p* only has one neighbor in its 3x3 space of neighboring boxes, we’ll call her n. Now *p* and *n* sit right next to each other in space, but we have no way of knowing whether they sit right next to one another in memory. The process says, “give me the particle p.” The CPU obliges and brings *p* along with all particles close to *p* that fit in the cache. Now the process says, “ok well the only particle that *p* has to care about is n.” If *n* is stored close to *p* in memory, great! She’s right there, no need to take the long trip back to off chip memory. But if *p* sits next to a different particle, we’ll call him m, in memory, we are out of luck. We have to go find *n* in memory again…and it can get worse. 

What if we bring *n* into the cache and to make room for her, kick out m? Then after calculating the force for *p* and *n* we want to calculate the force for m? Well now I have to go get *m* again! This is called cache thrashing (pretty metal I know) - where you keep replacing data you will need soon with data you need right now, resulting in more trips to memory than are necessary. But can we remedy this situation?

# Loop Tiling:
	
If your cache can’t hold anything bigger than 2 particles, you’re screwed. Lucky for us the L1 cache on modern machines is much larger, usually 64KB. If we know the number of particles we have, and how much space each one takes up in memory, and we know the size of our cache, we can actually grab particles in a smarter way and reduce the amount of cache thrashing our program does.  In our program we read the computers L1 cache size, and calculate the tiling size based on it. Where the tiling size is essentially what dictates what memory you access when. 

    const double cache_util = 0.45;
    int usable_cache = (int)(l1_cache_bytes * cache_util);
    int bytes_per_particle = PARTICLE_ATTRIBUTES * sizeof(double);
    int tile = usable_cache / bytes_per_particle;

    int power = 1;
    while(power < tile && power < 4096){
        power <<= 1;
    }
    tile = power >> 1;

    return (tile < 32) ? 32 : (tile > 2048) ? 2048 : tile;

Our tiling methodology can’t be bullet proof because at the end of the day, we could have particles in contiguous memory and particles in space that just don’t line up well. That is why we have the cache_util variable that basically says: try to be smart with about half the cache, and let the other half just operate how it needs to. 

# Back to the Sim:

Now with what I can only imagine was a mouth watering detour into cache coherency, we can start our simulation. For further performance gains our code is heavily inlined, and only allocates memory at the start. 

Basically after setting parameters (width, height, size of particle, box size, etc.) you choose a runtime and optionally give the simulation a premade matrix of particles. The sim then makes new safe random particles (if none were provided), creates the nodes to wrap them in and figures out where in our grid to place them. After that it begins running the same code over and over for some number of timesteps until the runtime has been reached. Inside of this time loop, the sim does two main jobs. 

First: using a particle's acceleration, velocity and the size of the timestep, calculate and update its new position. Repeat for every particle. 

Second: Using the position of a particle and that of all its neighbors, calculate the force and update its new velocity and acceleration. 

The fact that position relies on velocity and acceleration, and vice versa, means that you cannot update just one particle's positional or motional attributes. You must move all the particles in one big step, and then move onto the next computation.

The sim does some additional tasks like saving all the particles’ attributes for a timestep to be able to be graphed later. It also adaptively updates the timestep which for me was the hardest part. 

# Timesteppin’:

If you recall what I said about potentials, you’ll remember that too small a timestep is boring, and too large a step will explode the simulation. What we’ve done is made a timestep that skips the boring parts, and avoids the terrible explosion. It’s essentially like fast forwarding through Endgame to see this: 

![](images/cap.jpg)

But moving onto the next movie before seeing this:

![](images/rdj.webp, width="50%")

We do this by finding the “worst case scenario” particles. That is, first we find the shortest distance between two particles in our simulation, then we find the particle that is moving the fastest. Then we say, “imagine if these two close particles were moving that fast, how long would we have until they touch, and things start to go crazy.” The neat thing is this doesn’t add to our time complexity since A) we had to look at all the particles, so we are guaranteed to have already seen the fastest and B) The two closest particles are guaranteed to be neighbors, and we will have used their distance in our force calculation. From there it’s just a matter of keeping track of that data. 

# Results:

So there you have it, a cache coherent, memory efficient, adaptive molecular dynamics simulation written in pure C. I know your mascara is running from the myriad of positive emotions you’re feeling, but wipe away those tears honey because we gotta look at some plots! 

Taran and I have found that the adaptive timestep suffers from minor power drift with larger particle densities and longer runtimes. But where it stumbles in accuracy loss I think it more than makes up for in efficiency. You can run a 5000 particle simulation in a 100x100 area for a runtime of a minute in around ten minutes. Meanwhile without the adaptive timestep we ran it for 45 minutes and it still didn’t get close to finishing.
