## Hello Levi, 

I arrived home to New Mexico at 11:50 p.m  on the 19th of May. So for a short time before you jettison off to Squamish we share the sun's heat. I left Greece at 7 a.m on the 19th and after 25 hours of travelling, it was still the 19th. 

Time has its quirks.

Now I must combat the onslaught of jet lag that has cursed me for chasing the sun westward. I hope writing this will offer me a means to that end. 

In honor of this terrible commute, today I want to reflect on all the commuting I did. Moving myself to, through and from Greece was no easy task, and having no prior experience in doing so, Emily and I made a few mistakes. Travel days brought out levels of anxiety I haven’t faced outside of final exams and trad run outs, but now that I rest safely at home, I feel I can look back on them with minor fondness.

I’ve discussed in a previous piece the trials of getting there, but those paled in comparison to being there. The first stumble was when Apple Maps told us that we should be waiting for our bus to Delphi on some random street corner. After no bus appeared, we had to take a cab to the bus terminal that Google Maps pointed us to where we had to spend more money on new tickets out of Athens. Much to my dismay (and I’m sure to your pleasure) Google Maps dwarfs Apple Maps abroad. 

The next struggle was the bus-train-bus trip from Meteora to Volos. While we booked the entire trip through Hellenic Trains, and we didn’t miss any connection, the day was still a brutal one. Seven hours of minor discomfort, anxiety about making connections, and getting a lot of peering eyes got to me by the end of the day. I’m unsure if it’s insensitive to complain about being stared at as a white man, but it did make me feel a bit odd. Not to say that anyone was being rude, but it made me feel very much like a naive American. This was the day that I had my first and only cheat meal of the trip. At 11 p.m in Volos, the few places open only served meat. I ate two chicken gyros, they were life changing, I regret nothing.

By far the most exciting hiccup was on our commute back to Athens from Skopelos; an island and our last destination on the trip. Our ferry arrived at Agnondas, the town across the island, because the Skopelos port (the main town named after the island) was closed. I had received emails about it and planned for it; no harm no foul. However, at around 10:40 a.m on the day we were supposed to leave  the island, the ferry company wasn’t as kind. I thought we were supposed to leave from Skopelos since I hadn’t got an email saying otherwise, and in anticipation of a leisurely morning,  I hadn’t gotten our physical tickets yet. Having an hour before the ferry was supposed to leave, I stopped by a kiosk to get the tickets printed. It wasn’t until then that I was informed that our ferry was once again leaving from Agnondas. 

I walked outside eyeing the bus that was to leave in 20 minutes; our last chance to make it on time. Rushing into the nearby coffee shop I frantically explained to Emily that the leisurely hour we have to pack and come down to the port had been cut to 20 minutes and we needed to run. We began running the ~½ mile uphill to our hotel, both of us sporting swimsuits and crocs. We pack our bags and run out the door, less than 10 minutes until the bus leaves. Tearing out into the street I see a woman driving with her window down. I yell. She stops. I try to explain to her our situation. Before I finish she has the trunk open, we are throwing our bags inside and cramming into the backseat with her toddler. Zooming through the winding road, this superhero in women's clothing gets us to the bus with a couple minutes to spare. I try to give her money, she refuses and explains to the driver our situation. 

Sweaty, panting and still in swimsuits, Emily and I board the bus. We make it to the ferry and eventually, after another miserable bus ride, get to Athens where Dimitrios (our lovely cab driver) takes us to our final hotel of the trip in Artemida.

The nights before travel days I hardly slept. My mind ran afoul thinking about all the things that could go wrong, and all the money we’d be out of if we missed something. All day I’d double check boarding times, locations, and seating charts looking for errors that were never there. Since I was mostly sitting I hardly ate, and felt stiff the next day. It was by far the worst aspect of an otherwise amazing trip.

The bright side, we’re learning. Emily and I agree that next time, we want to stay in places longer, and cut down on these days of commute misery.

I hope you enjoy your commute northward to Canada, and I look forward to hearing about it.
